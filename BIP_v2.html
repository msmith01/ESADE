Financial Statements

  • The balance sheet includes assets (goods and producing assets) and financing sources (equity, debt, and reinvestments from net earnings) as of a point in time.
  • The income statement is a measure of operations, the activities involved in selling the goods and services: EBITDA, EBIT, net income…
  • The statement of cash flows reports the cash inflows and outflows associated with the operating, investing, and financing activities of the business.
  • The statement of shareholders’ equity keeps track of the investments made by the shareholders—either through equity investments or reinvested earnings—reduced by dividends paid to the shareholders.

Financial Statements

  • Balance Sheet
    • Summarizes a company’s assets, liabilities and shareholders’ equity at one moment in time.
    • Give stakeholders an idea as to what the company owns and owes, as well as the amount invested by the shareholders.
  • A standard company Balance Sheet has three parts:
    • Assets; what the company owns. Anything tangible (cash, trade receivables, inventories, buildings, equipment, etc.) or intangible (copyrights, goodwill, trademarks, patents,…) that is capable of being owned or controlled to produce value and that is held to have positive economic value.
    • Liabilities are an organization’s responsibilities that come from past actions or events. To settle these liabilities, the organization may have to give up assets, provide services, or do something that results in an economic cost in the future.
    • Shareholders’ equity; represents the remaining interest in assets of a company, after all liabilities are paid.
  • Balance Sheet is a snapshot (STATIC picture) of the company’s financial position at one point in time (a single moment in time).

Accounting Ledger

Accounting Ledger (journal entries)

  • A ledger account is a record that contains all transactions in a company’s financial records.
  • Contains all information about an account’s opening and closing balances.
  • Contains information on periodical debit and credit adjustments based on daily journal entries.
  • Essential when constructing a company’s financial statements.

Accounting Ledger

Debit

  • Debit is an entry made on the left side of an account.
  • Increases an asset or expenses
  • Decreases equity, liabilities or revenue accounts

Credit

  • Credit is an entry made on the right side of an account.
  • Increases equity, liabilities or revenue accounts
  • Decreases an asset or expenses.

Financial Ratios

Liquidity Ratios

  • Current Ratio: Measures a company’s ability to cover short-term liabilities with short-term assets.
    • Definition: Calculated as Current Assets / Current Liabilities. A higher ratio indicates better liquidity.
  • Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio): Evaluates the company’s ability to pay short-term obligations without relying on inventory sales.
    • Definition: Calculated as (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities. This ratio excludes inventory from current assets, providing a more conservative liquidity measure.

Solvency Ratios

  • Total Liabilities to Total Assets Ratio: Measures a company’s ability to meet its liabilities using its assets. A higher ratio suggests the company relies more on debt to finance its operations, indicating greater financial leverage.
    • A higher ratio indicates that a company is heavily reliant on debt. If the company faces declining revenues or economic downturns, it may struggle to meet debt repayments, potentially leading to financial distress or bankruptcy.
    • Reduced Flexibility: High leverage limits a company’s ability to take on additional debt - i.e. no bank will be willing to lend them more money!
    • Higher Interest Costs: With more debt, the company will incur higher interest expenses - higher costs for the company
    • Creditworthiness Concerns: Creditors and investors often view a high liabilities-to-assets ratio as a sign of potential instability, which can lead to lower credit ratings
  • Debt to Equity Ratio: Shows the proportion of debt relative to shareholder equity, indicating the financial leverage a company uses.
    • Definition: Calculated as Total Debt / Shareholder’s Equity. A higher ratio means a company is more reliant on debt for financing.
  • Interest Coverage Ratio: Assesses a company’s ability to cover its interest payments on outstanding debt.
    • Definition: Calculated as Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT) / Interest Expense. A higher ratio indicates greater ability to meet interest obligations.

Profitability Ratios

  • Net Profit Margin: Represents how much profit a company makes for every dollar of revenue, showing operational efficiency.
    • Definition: Calculated as Net Income / Revenue. A higher margin means the company retains more profit from its sales.
  • Return on Assets (ROA): Measures how efficiently a company uses its assets to generate profit.
    • Definition: Calculated as Net Income / Total Assets. A higher ROA indicates better asset utilization for generating income.

Efficiency Ratios

  • Asset Turnover Ratio: Indicates how efficiently a company uses its assets to generate revenue.
    • Definition: Calculated as Net Sales / Average Total Assets. A higher ratio means the company generates more revenue per dollar of assets.
  • Inventory Turnover Ratio: Shows how many times inventory is sold and replaced during a period, reflecting inventory management efficiency.
    • Definition: Calculated as Cost of Goods Sold / Average Inventory. A higher turnover means faster inventory movement.

Market Value Ratios

  • Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio: Compares a company’s current share price to its earnings per share, helping investors evaluate stock value.
    • Definition: Calculated as Market Price per Share / Earnings per Share (EPS). A higher P/E suggests the stock is more expensive relative to earnings.
  • Dividend Yield: Measures the dividend income relative to the price of a company’s shares, indicating the return an investor receives in dividends.
    • Definition: Calculated as Annual Dividends per Share / Market Price per Share. A higher yield means better return on investment through dividends.
